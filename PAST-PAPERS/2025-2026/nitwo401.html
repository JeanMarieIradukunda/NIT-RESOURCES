20. Packet loss is defined as the number of data packets transmitted from one point in a network but dropped during transmission and never reaching their destination. Analyze five (5) impacts of packet loss on overall network performance.
10 Marks
Key Evaluation Benchmarks:
  1. Throughput Degradation: TCP shrinks window size, lowering transmission speeds.
  2. Latency & Retransmissions: Dropped frames force timeout/retransmission loops.
  3. Jitter & Media Fragmentation: Real-time packets arrive broken, causing audio/video stuttering.
  4. Application Session Timeouts: Dropped packets can drop active database/SSH links.
  5. Overhead Cost Amplification: Retransmissions increase bandwidth waste.

[From DSC_0049.jpg] 22. One network records a signal strength of -41 dBm with a noise level of -50 dBm, while another network records the same signal strength of -41 dBm but with a noise level of -96 dBm.
15 Marks
a) What will be their signal-to-noise ratio?
b) Compare the resulting signals to noise ratio of the two different network and decide which is greater or less.
c) Evaluate the impact of environmental factors on an outdoor wireless network and suggest measures to mitigate these issues.
Analytical Engineering Solutions:
Formula: $\text{SNR} = \text{Signal Strength (dBm)} - \text{Noise Level (dBm)}$
Network 1 SNR: $-41 - (-50) = 9\text{ dB}$ (Very low/poor quality)
Network 2 SNR: $-41 - (-96) = 55\text{ dB}$ (Excellent throughput quality)
Comparison: Network 2's SNR is much higher ($55\text{ dB} > 9\text{ dB}$), meaning cleaner transmission speeds.
Mitigations: Tree trimming, line of sight optimization, protective outdoor enclosures, shifting frequency spectrum channels.
